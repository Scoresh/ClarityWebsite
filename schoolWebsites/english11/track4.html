Analysis / Reasoning:

This song, along with being catchy and a key staple of 1980’s Euro-Disco, also represents Holden Caulfield's journey through New York: namely his encounter with the prostitute and dancing with Bernice, Marty, and Laverne. The first five lines of Mr. Zivago’s Little Russian describe a lonely man. “Looking back, You can remember how the time is gone. The empty mirror when you are left alone. The night is gonna be keeping. Something in your mind.” (Zivago 1-5). Since The Catcher in the Rye is written as a memoir and a flashback from Holden Caulfield’s perspective, these lyrics make sense. Holden is left mostly alone in the mental hospital - and he has his experience in New York City on his mind. The chorus of Little Russian describes a series of events separated by “Little Russian.” “Live just for a night … You’re looking for a love tonight … Dancing a longer night … You’re jumping again tonight … Just to be free and wild.” (Zivago 10-24). Each line describes Holden’s first day in New York. Essentially, Holden wants to experience the life of an adult (but then, of course, he realizes that he wants to be the catcher). Holden calls the girl from Princeton “looking for a love tonight.” He jumps and dances with the three girls in the Wicker bar. Little Russian and Holden’s journey share this teenage dream of escaping and becoming an adult; the first thing a child wants is staying up late. The teenage dream of breaking away and “dancing the night away” is clearly defined both in the song and the book.
