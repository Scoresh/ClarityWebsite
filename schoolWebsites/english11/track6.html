Analysis / Reasoning:

Forever Young, by Alphaville, is this cliche song about staying young, tying directly into Caulfield’s idea of becoming the catcher in the rye. “‘Anyway, I keep picturing all these little kids playing some game in this big field of rye and all. Thousands of little kids, and nobody's around–nobody big, I mean–except me. And I’m standing on the edge of some crazy cliff.” (Salinger 191) The first stanza of Forever Young describes a pair of people dancing, only watching the skies, but worrying if their dance will be interrupted. This correlates to Holden’s idea of the catcher in the rye, shielding kids from running off the cliff. In Holden’s case, he is protecting the pair of people from worrying about “dropping the bomb or not.” Childhood is an intricate thing; children often yearn to grow up. However, this is different. Both the song and the book defy expectations on how youth is perceived. Rather than embracing maturity, Holden, like this song, romanticizes this youth as a fragile and innocent state. The lyrics “Do you really want to live forever?” (Alphaville 14) poses a question that Holden Caulfield would not hesitate to answer. At the point in the novel where Holden is set on becoming the catcher in the rye, Holden would undoubtedly say “yes.”
