Analysis / Reasoning:

Voyage, Voyage by Desireless, is a French staple of 80’s music that truly describes the escape that Holden Caulfield is looking for. When Holden Caulfield goes on a date with Sally, he proposes an idea of escaping off the grid, “stay(ing) in these cabin camps and stuff like that till the dough runs out. … We could live somewhere with a brook and all and, later on, we could get married or something.” (Salinger 146-147) This quote can directly be juxtaposed with the themes that Desireless is trying to convey. Though most of the song talks about different places in the world, Desireless makes sure to include sly details that can be interpreted in different ways. For example, the chorus is: “Travel, further in the night and day. Travel, in spaces unheard of by love. Travel, on the sacred waters of an Indian river. Travel, and never come back.” (Desireless 9-12). The chorus portrays an innocence that Holden wants to pursue. Holden desires to travel to spaces unheard of (remote areas in Vermont), travel on the sacred waters of an Indian river (“somewhere with a brook and all”), and wants to never come back (“we could get married or something”). While the song itself plays a cheerful tone, the 7th lyric provides a twist. The line, “Above the capitals, fatal ideas,” (Desireless 7) introduces an underlying tone that mirrors Holden’s dangerous thoughts. Though Holden’s fantasies are of innocence and of teenage dreams, his ideas themselves could be more desperate and “fatal,” just like the warning that Desireless sings about. So, Desireless completely describes Holden’s desires.
